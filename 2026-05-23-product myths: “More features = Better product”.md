One of the biggest product myths: “More features = Better product.”

But real user behavior says the opposite.

People love the idea of having more options…
until they actually have to use them.

Then suddenly:
• Too many buttons become confusing
• Too many settings become stressful
• Too many choices become exhausting
This is called the Paradox of Choice.

The more options users face, the harder decision-making becomes. Eventually, users stop exploring, delay decisions, or abandon the product entirely.

That’s why some of the world’s best products feel incredibly simple.
🟢 Apple removed complexity.
🟢 Google simplified search.
🟢 Netflix focuses heavily on recommendation instead of overwhelming navigation.

Because great UX is not about adding everything users ask for.

It’s about understanding:
“What is the ONE thing the user really needs right now?”

A feature may look valuable before launch. But after real usage, usability almost always matters more than capability. This is where many products slowly fail.
🔴 Feature by feature.
🔴 Screen by screen.
🔴 Until the product becomes bloated, noisy, and mentally tiring.

Sometimes the best product decision is not: “What should we add next?”
It’s: “What should we remove?”

Simple products are harder to build.

Because simplicity requires clarity, prioritization, restraint, and confidence.
Anyone can add features. Very few teams know what NOT to build.
