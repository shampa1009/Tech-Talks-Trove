“If the overall design is good, small details don’t matter.”

In reality, details ARE the design.

As Charles Eames said, “The details are not the details. They make the design.”

And some of the biggest business wins in tech came from surprisingly small UX changes.

A few real examples:
• Best Buy reportedly generated an additional $300 million/year after simplifying checkout by allowing users to purchase without mandatory account creation (“The $300 Million Button”).
• ClickTale nearly doubled sign-up conversions simply by adding the word “optional” beside a phone number field.
• A small improvement in e-commerce error messages reportedly increased completed purchases by 0.5% — saving over £250,000 annually.

None of these changes redesigned the entire product.
They optimized moments.

That’s the part many teams underestimate: Users experience products through micro-interactions.

A loading state.
An empty state.
A reassuring error message.
A button label.
A smooth animation.
A confirmation toast.
The order of products on a page.

Tiny details can quietly influence:
• Trust
• Conversion
• Retention
• Perceived quality
• Emotional connection

This is why companies like Apple obsess over details most people barely notice.

Because users may not consciously remember every interaction, but they absolutely remember how the product made them feel.

Great products are rarely just “feature-rich.”
They feel thoughtful. And thoughtful products are built through hundreds of tiny details done exceptionally well.

One of my favorite references for this mindset: “Little Big Details” — a collection showing how small UX decisions create delightful experiences.
