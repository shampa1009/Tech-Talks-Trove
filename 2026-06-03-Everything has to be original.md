Everything has to be original.

In reality, users don’t care about originality nearly as much as they care about clarity and usability.

Many designers try to reinvent common UI patterns just to make something look “unique.”
But most design conventions exist for a reason. They’ve already been tested by millions of users.

Search bars look familiar.
Cart icons behave predictably.
Navigation patterns repeat across apps.

And that’s a good thing. Because every time users open a product, they shouldn’t have to relearn how the interface works.

As Don't Make Me Think famously suggests: “Conventions are your friends.”

Great product design is often less about inventing new interactions, and more about reducing friction.
Originality becomes valuable only when it genuinely improves the user experience.

Otherwise, “creative” interfaces can quietly increase:
• Confusion
• Cognitive load
• Onboarding friction
• Drop-offs

Sometimes the smartest design decision is not asking: “How can we make this different?”
But asking: “How can we make this instantly understandable?”

Because users usually remember how easy our product felt; not how creatively we redesigned the button.
