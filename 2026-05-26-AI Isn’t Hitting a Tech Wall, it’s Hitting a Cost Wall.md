AI Isn’t Hitting a Tech Wall, it’s Hitting a Cost Wall.

One of the biggest shifts happening in AI right now is not about model quality.
It’s about economics.

For the past year, companies rushed to adopt AI coding tools like Claude Code and GitHub Copilot, expecting massive productivity gains. And the gains were real.

But there’s a hidden problem: The more useful AI agents become, the more expensive they are to operate.

A recent report highlighted that Microsoft is quietly reducing internal Claude Code usage and moving teams toward GitHub Copilot CLI — not because the tools failed, but because the token costs became difficult to justify at scale.

Another striking example came from Uber: Their CTO revealed that the company exhausted its planned 2026 AI coding budget in just four months, with some engineers reportedly consuming $500–$2,000/month in AI tokens.

This changes how we should think about AI products. AI is no longer behaving like traditional SaaS software with fixed seat pricing.
It’s starting to behave more like:
• Electricity
• Cloud infra
• Utility billing

The meter keeps running while the model “thinks.” And agentic systems think A LOT.
That’s why the next phase of AI adoption will likely focus less on: “Can AI do this?” 
And more on: “Can we economically sustain this at enterprise scale?”

The productivity upside is real.
But now finance teams, product teams, and engineering leaders all have to sit at the same table.

Because in AI, better intelligence currently comes with higher operational burn.

And that may become one of the defining product and business challenges of this decade.
