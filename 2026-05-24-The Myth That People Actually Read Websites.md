The Myth That People Actually Read Websites

Most users scan first. Then decide whether something is worth reading.
People are usually looking for:
• A keyword
• A heading
• A button
• A specific answer
• Something visually easy to process

That’s why huge paragraphs, complicated instructions, and cluttered interfaces often fail online.

Jakob Nielsen’s eye-tracking research found that users read less than 20% of the text on an average webpage.

Even more interesting? Studies show people often share articles they never fully read. This changes how we should design digital products.

Good web content is not written like a textbook. It’s structured for scanning.
✅ Meaningful headings
✅ Short paragraphs
✅ Highlighted keywords
✅ Clean hierarchy
✅ Scannable lists
✅ Clear calls to action

The goal is NOT to make users read more. The goal is to help users find value faster. Ironically, the easier content is to scan, the more likely people are to actually read it.

The best interfaces don’t demand attention. They guide it.
