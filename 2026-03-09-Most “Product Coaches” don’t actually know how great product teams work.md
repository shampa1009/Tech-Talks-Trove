Most “Product Coaches” don’t actually know how great product teams work.

After reading about what separates a good product coach from a bad one, one thing became clear: coaching product teams requires far more than teaching frameworks.

A great product coach typically does these things well:
• Has real hands-on experience working in strong product organizations.
 • Asks great questions and listens deeply instead of lecturing.
 • Focuses on developing people, not doing the work for them.
 • Understands both customer value and business outcomes.
 • Uses the right techniques for the situation, not forcing a favorite framework.
 • Creates safe spaces to practice and learn.
 • Gives honest, constructive feedback.
 • Empowers teams to solve problems independently.

In the end, the best product coaches measure success not by activities or frameworks used, but by the outcomes teams achieve and the growth they demonstrate.

Great coaching doesn’t create dependency.
It creates capable, empowered product teams.
